The Problem
Capital Inefficiency
Same asset, wildly different price
Korean preferred shares carry the same economic rights as common shares — same dividends, same residual claims — yet trade at discounts of 30–70%. When companies buy back common shares instead, they destroy shareholder value with every transaction.
Governance Failure
Control transfer without paying a premium
Common share buybacks reduce total share count, automatically increasing the controlling shareholder's stake — without paying a control premium. This transfers voting power from minority shareholders to the controlling family at all shareholders' expense.
Conflict of Interest
Boards benefit personally from common buybacks
Executive compensation is tied to common share performance. Boards therefore have a direct personal incentive to buy common shares — not preferred — regardless of what is optimal for all shareholders. This is a textbook fiduciary duty conflict.
Minority Shareholder Dilution
Your vote is worth less every quarter
As common shares are cancelled, the controlling shareholder's voting weight rises automatically. A 30% stake becomes 40%, then 50% — with no premium paid, no shareholder approval sought. Minority voting influence is systematically eroded.
The Proof — Companies Said So Themselves
"Preferred shares meet the definition of common shares as stipulated in K-IFRS 1033 'Earnings Per Share'."
— Hyundai Motor Company, Annual Report (Official Filing)
"Preferred shares have no preferential rights to profit distribution or residual assets, and therefore meet the definition of common shares under K-IFRS 1033."
— SK Corporation, Annual Report (Official Filing)
The implication: These companies have officially declared that preferred shares are economically equivalent to common shares under accounting standards — yet continue to buy common shares at 30–70% premium. Their own filings condemn their own capital allocation decisions.

Legal & Accounting Basis
Korean Commercial Code
Prof. Ok-ryul Song, Commercial Law: "The economic substance of 1% preferred shares is clearly that of non-voting common shares. They are simply common shares issued without voting rights."
K-IFRS 1033 / KASB
Korea Accounting Standards Board official interpretation: Legacy preferred shares satisfy the definition of common shares under K-IFRS 1033 paragraph 5 and must be treated accordingly in EPS calculations.
Fiduciary Duty
Korean Supreme Court (2005Da41716): Grossly irrational decisions are excluded from business judgment rule protection. Buying expensive common when cheap preferred exists — with companies' own filings confirming equivalence — may constitute a breach.
The Governance Problem
Control Transfer Without Paying a Control Premium

In a normal market transaction, acquiring control requires paying a premium above market price. Korean controlling families are achieving the same result — increasing their voting weight from 30% to 40% to 50%+ — using company funds, with no premium paid, no shareholder vote, and no disclosure of the governance consequences. This is not a side effect. For some boards, it may be the point.

What We Are Asking
01
Disclose the rationale
Boards must publicly explain why common shares are selected over preferred when a significant price gap exists. Silence is not governance.
02
Buy preferred first
When preferred shares trade at material discounts, capital efficiency demands preferred buybacks. Same economics, lower cost — the choice is mathematically obvious.
03
Legislate the standard
Amend the Financial Investment Services Act to require boards to disclose and justify share class selection in buyback programs. Make the conflict of interest visible.
